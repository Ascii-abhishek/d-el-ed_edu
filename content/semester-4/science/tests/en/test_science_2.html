3. What are the aims of first aid?

The three main aims of first aid are: (1) Preserve life — prevent death by maintaining breathing and circulation; (2) Prevent worsening — stop the injury or illness from becoming more serious; (3) Promote recovery — reduce pain, suffering, and ensure the person reaches professional medical care.

4. What is the function of haemoglobin? What happens when its level falls?

Haemoglobin is the iron-containing protein in RBCs that binds oxygen in the lungs and transports it to all body tissues. It also carries some CO₂ back to the lungs. When haemoglobin falls below normal (anaemia), the body cannot deliver enough oxygen — causing weakness, fatigue, pale skin, breathlessness, and dizziness.

6. What are the three degrees of burns? Which is most serious?

1st degree: Epidermis only affected; red, dry skin; painful. 2nd degree: Epidermis and part of dermis; blisters; very painful. 3rd degree: All skin layers destroyed; charred/leathery appearance; no pain (nerves destroyed). Third degree is the most serious — it requires immediate hospital treatment and often skin grafting.

7. How is Hepatitis B transmitted? How is it prevented?

Transmission: Blood contact (transfusion, sharing needles), sexual contact with infected person, and from infected mother to newborn during delivery or breastfeeding. NOT transmitted through food, water, or casual contact.

Prevention: Vaccination (3-dose HBV vaccine — most effective); screening donated blood before transfusion; using sterile, disposable needles; safe sex practices.

8. Why is a compass needle useful for navigation?

A compass needle is a small, freely suspended permanent magnet. Earth has a magnetic field, and the needle aligns itself with this field — the north pole of the needle always points toward geographic north (because Earth's magnetic south pole is located near geographic north). This consistent alignment allows travelers to determine direction reliably from any location on Earth's surface.

9. What is the 30:2 rule in CPR and why is it important?

The 30:2 rule in CPR means giving 30 chest compressions followed by 2 rescue breaths, then repeating this cycle. This ratio balances the need to maintain blood circulation (compressions) with the need to provide oxygen (rescue breaths). Compressions must not be interrupted for too long — the 30:2 ratio minimises pauses while still providing ventilation. This ratio has been validated as the most effective for adult CPR.

10. What is magnetic declination and why must navigators account for it?

Magnetic declination is the angle between geographic north (true north, direction of Earth's geographic North Pole) and magnetic north (direction a compass needle points) at any given location. The two are not the same — they can differ by several degrees depending on location, and the difference changes slowly over time. Navigators must account for declination when using a compass for precise navigation, otherwise they would travel in the wrong direction. Ships, aircraft, and expedition teams calculate the local declination before using compass readings for course planning.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Describe blood structure — all four components with their functions and key facts.

Blood is a connective tissue with four components:

1. Plasma (~55% of blood): Straw-coloured liquid; 90% water. Functions: transports nutrients, hormones, CO₂, waste; contains antibodies; maintains pH and temperature. Serum = plasma without clotting proteins.

2. Red Blood Cells (RBCs / Erythrocytes, ~40–45%): Biconcave disc shape; no nucleus in mature form. Contains haemoglobin (iron protein) that carries O₂. Count: 4.5–5.5 million/mm³. Lifespan: 120 days. Made in red bone marrow. Low RBC/Hb = anaemia.

3. White Blood Cells (WBCs / Leucocytes, <1%): Irregular shape; have nucleus. 5 types: neutrophils (60–70%, fight bacteria), lymphocytes (20–30%, antibodies), monocytes (3–8%, large phagocytes), eosinophils (2–4%, parasites), basophils (0.5–1%, allergy). Count: 4,000–11,000/mm³.

4. Platelets (Thrombocytes, <1%): Tiny cell fragments; no nucleus; 7–10 day lifespan. Count: 1.5–4 lakhs/mm³. Function: blood clotting — form platelet plug, trigger fibrin formation to seal wounds.

2. Explain the DRABC protocol and CPR in detail.

DRABC Primary Survey:

  • D — Danger: Assess scene for hazards (electric wires, gas, traffic, fire). Ensure your safety and bystander safety before approaching victim.
  • R — Response: Shake shoulders gently and shout "Are you okay?" If no response → unconscious.
  • A — Airway: Head-tilt, chin-lift to open airway. Check for/remove visible obstruction.
  • B — Breathing: Look-listen-feel for 10 seconds. If no breathing → 2 rescue breaths (pinch nose, seal mouth, blow until chest rises).
  • C — Circulation: Check carotid pulse for 10 seconds. If no pulse → start CPR. Call emergency services.

CPR Procedure:

  1. Person on firm surface. Kneel beside chest.
  2. Heel of hand on centre of chest (lower sternum), second hand on top.
  3. Push down ≥5 cm at 100–120 per minute. Allow full recoil.
  4. After 30 compressions → 2 rescue breaths.
  5. Repeat 30:2 cycle until help arrives, victim recovers, or rescuer exhausted.
3. Explain Earth's magnetism — causes, magnetic poles, declination, and dip.

Cause: Earth's magnetic field is generated by convection currents in the molten iron-nickel outer core. Moving charged material creates electric currents which produce a magnetic field — the geodynamo effect.

Magnetic poles: Earth has a magnetic North Pole (actually near geographic south, Antarctica) and magnetic South Pole (near geographic north, Canada). The compass N-pole points toward geographic north because Earth's magnetic S-pole is there.

Geographic vs Magnetic North: They are about 11° apart. Earth's rotational axis (geographic) ≠ magnetic axis.

Declination: Angle between geographic north and magnetic north at any location. Positive (east) or negative (west) depending on location. Essential for accurate navigation.

Angle of Dip (Inclination): Angle the Earth's field makes with horizontal. 0° at magnetic equator (field is horizontal); 90° at magnetic poles (field is vertical/straight down). Intermediate values elsewhere.

4. Explain AIDS — transmission, stages, prevention, and how to combat stigma in schools.

Causative agent: HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) → causes AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome).

Transmission: (1) Unprotected sexual contact; (2) Contaminated blood/transfusion; (3) Sharing needles; (4) Mother to child (pregnancy, birth, breastfeeding). NOT by mosquitoes, casual contact, food, saliva.

Stages:

  1. Acute HIV (2–4 weeks): flu-like symptoms, high viral load.
  2. Clinical latency (years): virus active but slow; may be asymptomatic.
  3. AIDS: CD4 count <200/mm³; opportunistic infections (TB, pneumonia, fungal).

Prevention: Condoms; test blood before transfusion; sterile needles; ART during pregnancy.

Treatment: ART (antiretroviral therapy) — suppresses virus, prevents AIDS; lifelong; not a cure.

Combating stigma in schools: Teachers must correct myths — HIV is NOT spread by sharing food, classroom contact, or mosquitoes. Students with HIV+ parents or relatives should not face discrimination. Classroom discussions on empathy and science-based facts prevent fear-based prejudice. The goal is health literacy, not stigma.

5. Write a comprehensive first aid guide for a school teacher covering: burns, fractures, bleeding, and electric shock.

Burns:

  • Cool with running water 10–20 minutes. Remove jewellery near burn. Cover with sterile non-stick dressing.
  • DO NOT: apply butter, toothpaste, ice, or tight bandages. Do not burst blisters.
  • 3rd degree burns — hospital emergency immediately; cover with clean cloth only.

Fractures:

  • Keep victim still. Immobilise with splint (newspaper, stick) or sling (triangular bandage for arm).
  • Apply ice pack (wrapped) to reduce swelling. Elevate if possible.
  • DO NOT: move spine/neck injury victim; try to straighten bone; remove embedded objects.

Bleeding:

  • Apply firm direct pressure with clean cloth. Do not remove soaked cloth — add more on top.
  • Elevate injured part above heart level.
  • Arterial bleeding (bright red, spurting) = emergency; call ambulance; apply pressure point.

Electric Shock:

  • DO NOT touch victim while in contact with live electricity.
  • Switch off power source immediately, OR use dry wooden stick to separate victim from wire.
  • Start DRABC and CPR if needed. Treat burns at contact points. Call emergency services.

General rule: Always call an adult and emergency services (108) — first aid stabilises, not replaces, medical treatment.
