Section 2: Short Answer Questions (10 Questions)

1. Why are producers placed at the base of an ecological pyramid?

Producers are placed at the base because they make food by photosynthesis and support all consumer levels above them. They are the most numerous organisms and hold the most energy. Each level above has fewer organisms and less energy (10% rule — only 10% energy passes from one level to the next).

2. Why is concentration important in metallurgy?

Concentration removes unwanted impurities called gangue (sand, clay, rocky material) from the ore. This is important because gangue would interfere with the later chemical reduction step, waste energy, and produce impure metal. Concentration makes subsequent extraction steps more efficient and economical.

3. What is electronegativity and how does it change across a period?

Electronegativity is the tendency of an atom to attract a shared pair of electrons in a covalent bond. It increases across a period (left to right) because the number of protons in the nucleus increases while the number of electron shells stays the same — the greater nuclear charge pulls electrons more strongly toward itself.

4. State Ohm's law. Calculate the resistance if voltage is 15V and current is 3A.

Ohm's law: At constant temperature, the current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference. V = I × R. Therefore R = V/I = 15/3 = 5 Ω.

6. Name two properties of metals and two properties of non-metals.

Metals: (1) Malleable — can be beaten into thin sheets; (2) Good conductors of heat and electricity.

Non-metals: (1) Brittle — break when hammered; (2) Poor conductors of heat and electricity (exception: graphite).

7. What is the difference between a period and a group in the periodic table?

A period is a horizontal row. The period number equals the number of electron shells. A group is a vertical column. Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons and therefore similar chemical properties.

8. Why are household appliances connected in parallel rather than in series?

In a parallel circuit: (1) Each appliance receives the full supply voltage (220V) and works at its rated power. (2) Appliances work independently — if one fails, others continue. (3) Individual switches can control each appliance separately. In series, voltage divides and if one appliance fails, all stop working.

9. What is adaptation? Give two examples from nature.

Adaptation is a feature or behaviour that helps an organism survive and reproduce better in its environment, developed through natural selection over many generations. Examples: (1) Thick fur of polar bears — insulates against extreme cold. (2) Cactus has thick stems that store water and spines instead of leaves to reduce water loss in desert.

10. What are the steps in extraction of metals from ores?

The main steps are: (1) Mining — ore extracted from earth; (2) Crushing and grinding — broken into fine powder; (3) Concentration — gangue removed; (4) Roasting/Calcination — ore heated to form metal oxide; (5) Reduction — metal oxide reduced using coke to get crude metal; (6) Refining — crude metal purified (electrolytic refining).

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. Explain the components of an ecosystem using a school garden as an example. Draw a food web.

Ecosystem definition: A functional unit where biotic (living) and abiotic (non-living) components interact.

In a school garden:

  • Producers: Grass, flowering plants, neem tree — make food by photosynthesis.
  • Primary consumers: Ants, caterpillars, butterflies, sparrows (eating seeds).
  • Secondary consumers: Lizards eating insects, larger birds eating smaller birds.
  • Decomposers: Bacteria and fungi breaking dead leaves in soil.
  • Abiotic components: Sunlight, rainwater, soil, air, temperature.

Food web:

   Grass → Caterpillar → Lizard → Eagle
     |           ↓
     |       Small bird → Eagle
     ↓
   Ant → Lizard → Eagle

Nutrient cycling: Dead organisms decomposed by bacteria → nutrients return to soil → absorbed by plants → cycle continues.

3. Explain the modern periodic table's structure and key trends in properties.

Structure:

  • 118 elements arranged in order of increasing atomic number.
  • 7 Periods (horizontal rows) — period number = number of electron shells.
  • 18 Groups (vertical columns) — group number (for main group) = number of valence electrons.
  • Blocks: s-block (Groups 1–2), p-block (Groups 13–18), d-block (Groups 3–12, transition metals), f-block (lanthanides, actinides).

Key trends:

4. Explain series and parallel circuits with advantages of each. Describe domestic wiring.

Series circuit: Components connected end-to-end. Same current through all; voltage divides; total resistance = R₁ + R₂ + ... If one fails, all fail. Advantage: simple; used for fuse connections.

Parallel circuit: Components connected across same two points. Same voltage across all; current divides; total resistance less than smallest R. If one fails, others work. Advantage: independent operation at full voltage; used in homes.

Domestic wiring:

  • Live (red/brown): Carries current from supply.
  • Neutral (black/blue): Returns current to supply.
  • Earth (green/yellow): Safety wire — diverts fault current to earth.
  • All appliances connected in parallel — each gets full 220V, works independently.
  • Main fuse/MCB at distribution board protects the whole circuit.
  • Individual fuses/switches protect each branch.
5. Compare the roles of decomposers in an ecosystem and refining in metallurgy. What do both processes have in common?

Decomposers in ecosystem:

  • Bacteria and fungi break down dead organic matter (dead plants, animals).
  • Complex organic molecules broken into simple minerals and nutrients.
  • Nutrients returned to soil, which are then absorbed by producers.
  • This maintains nutrient cycling — without decomposers, nutrients would remain locked in dead matter.
  • They clean the ecosystem of dead waste.

Refining in metallurgy:

  • Crude metal (after reduction) contains impurities.
  • Electrolytic refining: crude metal (anode) dissolves and pure metal deposits on cathode.
  • Impurities collect at bottom (anode mud) — some contain valuable metals (gold, silver).
  • Refining removes impurities to make metal pure and useful.

Common theme: Both processes convert a complex, impure, or "used" material into a purer, more usable form. Decomposers "refine" dead matter back to nutrients; metallurgical refining "refines" crude metal to pure metal. Both are essential to their respective cycles — one biological, one industrial.
