Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

2. Name the steps involved in extraction of metals from ores.

The main steps are: (1) Mining — ore is taken from earth; (2) Crushing and grinding — large pieces broken small; (3) Concentration — gangue removed; (4) Reduction — metal extracted from ore; (5) Refining — crude metal purified.

3. What is an alloy? Give two examples with their uses.

An alloy is a mixture of a metal with one or more other metals or non-metals. Examples: (1) Brass (copper + zinc) — used in taps and musical instruments; (2) Steel (iron + carbon) — used in construction and tools.

4. Give two physical properties of non-metals.

(1) Non-metals are generally poor conductors of heat and electricity (except graphite). (2) They are brittle — they break when hammered instead of flattening into sheets.

5. Why is copper used for making electric wires?

Copper is ductile (can be drawn into wires) and an excellent conductor of electricity. It allows current to flow easily with very little resistance, making it ideal for electrical wiring in homes and industries.

2. Explain the complete process of extraction of metals from ores.

Step-by-step extraction:

  • Mining: Ore is extracted from earth by open-cast or underground mining.
  • Crushing and grinding: Large lumps broken into fine powder to increase surface area.
  • Concentration: Gangue (sand, clay) removed using water, froth flotation, or magnetic separation.
  • Roasting/Calcination: Ore heated in air to remove moisture and convert to oxide form.
  • Reduction: Metal oxide reduced using coke (carbon) or electrolysis to get crude metal.
  • Refining: Crude metal purified by electrolytic refining or distillation to get pure metal.

Example: Iron extraction from hematite (Fe₂O₃) using a blast furnace — coke reduces iron oxide to iron.

3. Write a classroom activity to help students distinguish between metals and non-metals.

Activity: Sorting Metals and Non-metals

Materials needed: Iron nail, copper wire, aluminium foil, piece of chalk, pencil graphite, piece of sulphur, battery, bulb, wires.

Steps:

  1. Ask students to observe each item for shine (lustre).
  2. Try to bend/hammer: metals bend into sheet; non-metals break.
  3. Connect each item in a simple circuit — if bulb glows, it conducts electricity.
  4. Tap each item gently — metals produce a ringing sound (sonorous).
  5. Record findings in a table: Material / Shiny / Conducts / Malleable / Sound.

Learning: Students observe properties directly instead of only memorising them, and exceptions like graphite (conducts but is non-metal) create curiosity.

4. What are alloys? Explain their importance in daily life.

Alloy: A homogeneous mixture of a metal with other metals or non-metals, made to improve properties such as strength, corrosion resistance, or hardness.

AlloyComponentsUse
SteelIron + CarbonBuildings, vehicles, tools
Stainless steelIron + Chromium + NickelUtensils, surgical tools
BrassCopper + ZincTaps, musical instruments
BronzeCopper + TinStatues, medals, bells
SolderLead + TinJoining electric components

Alloys are important because pure metals alone often have limitations — steel is stronger than pure iron, stainless steel does not rust, brass is harder than pure copper.
