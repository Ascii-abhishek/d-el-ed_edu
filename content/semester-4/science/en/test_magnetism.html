Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. What is magnetic declination? Why is it important?

Magnetic declination is the angle between the geographic north (true north) and the magnetic north at any given location. It is important for navigation — ships, aircraft, and explorers must account for declination when using a compass to find true north, otherwise they may navigate in the wrong direction.

2. Name three ferromagnetic materials.

The three most common ferromagnetic materials are iron (Fe), nickel (Ni), and cobalt (Co). These materials are strongly attracted to magnets and can be magnetised themselves.

3. How can a simple compass be made using a needle?

Stroke a sewing needle 50 times in the same direction with one pole of a permanent magnet (always lifting the magnet back to the start before the next stroke). The needle gets magnetised. Place it gently on a small floating leaf or thin piece of cork in a bowl of water. The magnetised needle will align itself in the north–south direction, acting as a simple compass.

5. Why is Earth said to behave like a giant magnet?

Earth has a magnetic field surrounding it, generated by electrical currents in its molten outer iron core. This field has two magnetic poles (near the geographic poles), causes compass needles to align north–south, and deflects charged particles from the sun (solar wind). These properties are exactly like those of a giant bar magnet inside the Earth.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Explain Earth's magnetism: magnetic poles, declination, and angle of dip.

Earth's Magnetism: Earth behaves like a huge bar magnet. Its magnetic field is generated by convection currents in the molten iron-nickel outer core.

Magnetic poles: Earth has a magnetic north pole (located near geographic south pole, in Antarctica) and a magnetic south pole (near geographic north pole, Canada). This means a compass N-pole is attracted toward geographic north because Earth's magnetic south pole is there.

Magnetic declination: The angle between true geographic north and the direction of compass north at any location. It varies by location and changes over time. Navigators must correct for it.

Angle of dip (inclination): The angle the Earth's magnetic field makes with the horizontal surface. At the magnetic equator = 0° (field is horizontal). At the magnetic poles = 90° (field points straight down). At most other places, the angle is between 0° and 90°.

2. Describe the construction and working of an electromagnet with applications.

Construction:

  • Take a soft iron rod (core).
  • Wind many turns of insulated copper wire around it in the same direction.
  • Connect the wire ends to a battery.

Working: When current flows through the coil, a magnetic field is produced around it. The soft iron core gets magnetised and greatly amplifies the field. When current stops, the soft iron loses its magnetism immediately.

Strength depends on:

  • Number of coil turns (more turns → stronger).
  • Current strength (more current → stronger).
  • Core material (soft iron is best).

Applications: Electric bell, telephone earpiece, MRI machines, industrial cranes, relays (switches), loudspeakers, maglev trains.

3. Explain magnetic field lines and their properties.

Magnetic field lines are imaginary lines used to represent the pattern of a magnetic field. They show both the direction and strength of the field.

Properties:

  • They emerge from the North pole and enter the South pole outside the magnet.
  • Inside the magnet, they travel from South to North.
  • They form closed continuous loops.
  • They never intersect each other (if they did, it would mean two directions at one point — impossible).
  • Closer field lines = stronger magnetic field; farther apart = weaker field.
  • The field is strongest near the poles where lines are densest.

Classroom demonstration: Place a bar magnet under a paper and sprinkle iron filings. Tap gently — the filings arrange into curved lines showing the field pattern.

4. Design a classroom activity using magnets for primary school students.

Activity: Magnetic or Non-magnetic?

Objective: Students classify common materials as magnetic or non-magnetic.

Materials: One bar magnet, collection of objects — iron nail, copper coin, aluminium foil, paper clip, pencil, rubber, glass marble, plastic spoon, wooden stick.

Steps:

  1. Students predict which objects will be attracted to the magnet.
  2. Bring the magnet near each object and observe.
  3. Record results in a two-column table: Magnetic / Non-magnetic.
  4. Discuss: Why do iron and steel respond but copper and aluminium do not?

Extension: Test objects from two ends (N and S poles). Ask: does it matter which pole is used? (No — both attract ferromagnetic materials.)

Learning: Students discover that "metallic" does not mean "magnetic" — copper, aluminium, and gold are metals but not magnetic. Only iron, nickel, and cobalt (and their alloys) are ferromagnetic.
