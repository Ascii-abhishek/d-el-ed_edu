Safety and First Aid

Practical and scoring chapter. Exams ask about DRABC, burn degrees, fracture types, first aid steps for specific emergencies, and first-aid box contents. Learn the sequences — they are often asked as fill-in-the-blank or short answer.

What Is First Aid?

  • Definition: Immediate, temporary help given to an injured or ill person before professional medical treatment arrives.
  • Aims: Preserve life, prevent condition from worsening, promote recovery.
  • First aid does NOT replace medical treatment — it buys time.
  • The Golden Hour: The first hour after serious injury when proper care can save a life.

DRABC Protocol (Primary Survey)

  • D — Danger: Check scene for hazards (gas leak, fire, traffic, live wire). Ensure your safety first.
  • R — Response: Gently shake shoulders and call loudly. Is the person conscious?
  • A — Airway: Tilt head back, lift chin. Remove visible obstruction from mouth.
  • B — Breathing: Look (chest rise), listen (breath sounds), feel (airflow on cheek). Give 2 rescue breaths if not breathing.
  • C — Circulation: Check pulse (carotid artery on neck). If no pulse, start CPR immediately.

CPR (Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation)

  • CPR is used when a person has no heartbeat and/or is not breathing.
  • Steps:
    1. Place person flat on firm surface.
    2. Kneel beside the chest.
    3. Place heel of hand on centre of chest (on the lower half of sternum).
    4. Push down at least 5 cm deep at a rate of 100–120 compressions per minute.
    5. Give 30 compressions, then 2 rescue breaths. Repeat.
    6. Continue until: victim responds, professional help arrives, or rescuer is exhausted.
  • In children: Use two fingers for infants; one hand for small children.

  • Always cool burns with running water — NEVER use ice, butter, toothpaste, or oil.
  • Remove jewellery near burn area before swelling starts.
  • Do NOT burst blisters — risk of infection.

Fractures

  • Definition: A break or crack in a bone.

  • Signs of fracture: Pain, swelling, deformity, inability to move, crepitus (grating sound).
  • First aid: (1) Do NOT move the victim unless danger. (2) Immobilise the fracture using a splint (rolled newspaper, stick). (3) Apply ice pack to reduce swelling. (4) Elevate if possible. (5) Get to hospital.
  • For neck/spine injury: DO NOT MOVE — risk of paralysis.

Bleeding Control

  • Types:
    • Capillary — oozing; minor; apply light pressure.
    • Venous — steady dark-red flow; apply firm pressure and elevate.
    • Arterial — spurting bright-red; most dangerous; apply strong direct pressure; use pressure point if needed.
  • Steps: (1) Apply firm pressure with clean cloth. (2) Do NOT remove cloth — add more on top. (3) Elevate injured part above heart level. (4) For severe limb bleeding — tourniquet as last resort, note time applied.

Electric Shock

  • Warning: Do NOT touch the person while they are still in contact with electricity.
  • Steps:
    1. Switch off the power source immediately.
    2. If unable to switch off — use a dry wooden stick, rubber mat, or folded dry newspaper to push victim away from source.
    3. Check DRABC — start CPR if needed.
    4. Treat burns at entry and exit points.
    5. Call emergency medical services.

Drowning

  • Remove victim from water carefully (spine injury risk).
  • Place in recovery position if breathing.
  • Start CPR immediately if not breathing.
  • Do NOT hold upside down to drain water — it wastes time.
  • Keep warm (risk of hypothermia).

Snake Bite

  • Keep victim calm and still — movement spreads venom faster.
  • Immobilise the bitten limb (like a fracture) — keep below heart level.
  • Apply pressure bandage over bite area (not tourniquet).
  • Remove jewellery from affected area.
  • Transport to hospital immediately for antivenom.
  • DO NOT: Cut wound, suck venom, apply tourniquet, apply ice, give alcohol.

Heatstroke

  • Occurs when body temperature exceeds 40°C and the body cannot cool itself.
  • Symptoms: Hot dry skin (NO sweating), confusion or seizures, rapid strong pulse, unconsciousness.
  • First aid: Move to cool shaded area, remove excess clothing, apply cold wet cloths or ice to armpits/neck/groin, give water if conscious, fan the person, call medical help.
  • Distinguish: Heat exhaustion (heavy sweating, weakness, normal mental state) → rest + fluids; Heatstroke (no sweating, confusion) → emergency.

School Safety Rules for Teachers

  • Know the location of the school first-aid box — check contents regularly.
  • Post emergency numbers (ambulance 108, fire 101, police 100) visibly.
  • Teach students the difference between a scrape and a serious wound.
  • Conduct fire drills; know exit routes.
  • Electricity safety: no wet hands on switches; no overloaded sockets.
  • Lab safety: safety glasses, no running, proper chemical storage.

One-minute Revision

  • DRABC: Danger → Response → Airway → Breathing → Circulation.
  • CPR: 30 compressions + 2 breaths at 100–120/min.
  • Burns: cool with running water; 3rd degree = no pain = most serious.
  • Fracture: immobilise, do not set the bone, hospital.
  • Bleeding: firm pressure, elevate, do not remove soaked cloth.
  • Electric shock: switch off power FIRST; do not touch with bare hands.
  • Snake bite: immobilise, keep calm, hospital — do NOT cut or suck.

Common Exam Lines

  • First aid is the immediate help given to an injured or sick person before medical treatment.
  • CPR is given at 30 compressions to 2 breaths at a rate of 100–120 compressions per minute.
  • Third degree burns have no pain because all nerve endings are destroyed.
  • In case of electric shock, never touch the victim while they are in contact with the live wire.
  • A triangular bandage can be used as a sling to support a fractured arm.
