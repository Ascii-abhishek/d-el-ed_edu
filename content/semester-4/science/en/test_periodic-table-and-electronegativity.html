Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. What is the difference between a period and a group in the periodic table?

A period is a horizontal row in the periodic table. Elements in the same period have the same number of electron shells. A group is a vertical column. Elements in the same group have the same number of valence electrons and therefore show similar chemical properties.

2. What is electronegativity? How does it change across a period?

Electronegativity is the tendency of an atom to attract a shared pair of electrons toward itself in a covalent bond. It increases from left to right across a period because the number of protons increases, pulling electrons more strongly, while the number of electron shells remains the same.

3. What were the limitations of Mendeleev's periodic table?

(1) Could not explain position of hydrogen (placed in Group 1 but has properties of both metals and non-metals). (2) Anomalous pairs — elements with higher atomic mass placed before those with lower atomic mass (e.g., Ar before K). (3) Position of lanthanides and actinides not clear. (4) Isotopes cause confusion as they have same atomic mass as a different element.

4. What is the difference between ionisation energy and electronegativity?

Ionisation energy is the energy needed to completely remove one electron from a neutral atom. Electronegativity is the ability to attract shared electrons in a bond — the electron is not completely removed. Both increase across a period and decrease down a group.

5. Name the scientist who gave the modern periodic law and state that law.

Henry Moseley (1913) gave the Modern Periodic Law: "The properties of elements are periodic functions of their atomic numbers." This means that when elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic number, elements with similar properties recur at regular intervals.

3. What is the significance of group number and period number in the periodic table?

Period number: Tells us the number of electron shells (energy levels) an atom has. An element in Period 3 has 3 electron shells.

Group number: For main group (s and p block) elements, it tells us the number of valence electrons. Elements in Group 1 have 1 valence electron; Group 17 (halogens) have 7 valence electrons.

Significance:

  • Elements in the same group react similarly (e.g., all alkali metals react vigorously with water).
  • Group 18 elements (noble gases) have 8 valence electrons (except He = 2) — completely stable, unreactive.
  • Knowing group and period can predict valency, bonding type, and reactivity.
4. Explain how the periodic table can be used as a teaching tool in primary school science.

Introduction approach: Don't introduce all 118 elements at once. Start with familiar materials — iron, gold, copper, oxygen — and place them on the table.

Activities:

  • Element cards: name, symbol, atomic number. Students sort by metal/non-metal/metalloid.
  • Colour-code the table: metals (yellow), non-metals (green), noble gases (blue), metalloids (orange).
  • Find familiar elements: Na (salt), Ca (bones/chalk), Fe (iron nail), C (pencil, charcoal).
  • Ask: "Why do sodium and potassium both react violently with water?" — same group = same valence electrons = similar reactivity.

Value: Students understand patterns instead of memorising random facts. The table becomes a reference tool, not a list to be memorised.
