Electric Charge and Current

Coulomb's Law (Static Electricity)

  • Two charges of same sign (both + or both −) repel each other.
  • Two charges of opposite sign attract each other.
  • Force between charges: F = kQ₁Q₂ / r² — increases if charges are larger, decreases if distance increases.
  • Static electricity examples: comb attracting paper bits after rubbing hair; lightning (discharge of built-up charge).

Ohm's Law

  • Statement: At constant temperature, the current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference across it.
  • Formula: V = I × R (or I = V/R or R = V/I)

Electric Power

  • Power (P) = Voltage (V) × Current (I) → P = VI
  • Also: P = I²R and P = V²/R
  • Unit: Watt (W). 1000 W = 1 kilowatt (kW)
  • Electrical energy = Power × Time → Unit: kilowatt-hour (kWh) = 1 "unit" on electricity bill
  • Example: A 60 W bulb running for 10 hours uses 60 × 10 = 600 Wh = 0.6 kWh

Heating Effect of Electric Current

  • When current flows through a resistance wire, it generates heat.
  • Joule's law: Heat produced (H) = I²Rt (current squared × resistance × time)
  • Applications: electric heater, iron, toaster, filament bulb (tungsten filament).
  • Tungsten is used in bulbs because it has very high melting point (3422°C).

Domestic Wiring — Key Points

  • Three wires in household supply: Live (brown/red), Neutral (blue/black), Earth (green/yellow).
  • All appliances are connected in parallel so they work independently.
  • Main fuse/MCB protects the whole circuit.
  • Do NOT touch live wire — it carries 220V in India.
  • Electric meters (kWh meters) measure total electricity consumption.

Classroom Safety Rules

  • Never touch electric switches with wet hands.
  • Do not put objects into electric sockets.
  • Keep electric equipment away from water.
  • Switch off the main switch before repairing any electrical device.
  • If someone is getting a shock: do NOT touch them — switch off power or use a dry wooden stick to separate them.

One-minute Revision

  • V = IR — Ohm's law (Voltage = Current × Resistance).
  • Series: same current, voltage divides, total R increases.
  • Parallel: same voltage, current divides, total R decreases.
  • Homes use parallel circuits — one appliance stops, others keep working.
  • Fuse / MCB + earthing = safety against overcurrent and shock.
  • P = VI; Energy unit = kWh (unit on electricity bill).

Common Exam Lines

  • Ohm's law states that V = IR at constant temperature.
  • In a parallel circuit, total resistance is less than the smallest individual resistance.
  • Earthing protects against electric shock by providing a path for excess current to go into the earth.
  • Conductors allow free flow of electrons; insulators restrict electron flow.
