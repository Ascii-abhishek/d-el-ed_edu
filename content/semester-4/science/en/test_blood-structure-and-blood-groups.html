Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. What is haemoglobin? What happens when it is low?

Haemoglobin is an iron-containing protein found in Red Blood Cells. It binds oxygen in the lungs and carries it to all tissues of the body. It gives blood its red colour. When haemoglobin is low (below normal levels), the condition is called anaemia — the person suffers from weakness, fatigue, pale skin, and breathlessness.

3. What is erythroblastosis fetalis? How is it prevented?

Erythroblastosis fetalis is a condition where an Rh− mother carrying an Rh+ baby produces anti-Rh antibodies (after the first pregnancy). In the second Rh+ pregnancy, these antibodies cross the placenta and attack the baby's RBCs, causing severe anaemia and jaundice in the newborn. It is prevented by giving the mother an Anti-D (RhoGAM) injection within 72 hours of delivery, miscarriage, or any event where the baby's blood may mix with the mother's.

4. Name the five types of White Blood Cells and state their main functions.
  • Neutrophils (60–70%): Engulf and destroy bacteria (phagocytosis); first responders.
  • Lymphocytes (20–30%): Produce antibodies (B-cells) and destroy infected cells (T-cells).
  • Monocytes (3–8%): Large phagocytes; become macrophages in tissues.
  • Eosinophils (2–4%): Fight parasites; involved in allergic reactions.
  • Basophils (0.5–1%): Release histamine; involved in inflammation and allergic response.
5. Why must donated blood be tested before being used for transfusion?

Donated blood is tested before use for two reasons: (1) Blood group matching — to ensure ABO and Rh compatibility between donor and recipient, preventing dangerous transfusion reactions. (2) Disease screening — to check for HIV, Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C, Syphilis, and Malaria, ensuring that infected blood is not accidentally transferred to a healthy recipient.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Describe the composition of blood and the functions of its components.

Blood is a connective tissue that has four main components:

Component% in BloodKey Functions
Plasma~55%Transport nutrients, hormones, CO₂, waste; maintains pH; contains antibodies
Red Blood Cells (RBCs)~40–45%Transport oxygen via haemoglobin; remove CO₂
White Blood Cells (WBCs)<1%Immunity — fight bacteria, viruses, parasites; produce antibodies
Platelets<1%Blood clotting — form platelet plug; trigger fibrin formation

Overall functions of blood: Transport O₂ and nutrients to cells; carry CO₂ and waste away; regulate body temperature; maintain pH; defend against infection; clot to prevent blood loss.

3. Describe the process of blood clotting step by step.

Blood clotting (Haemostasis):

  1. Vascular spasm: Blood vessel contracts to reduce blood flow.
  2. Platelet plug: Platelets gather at the wound site and stick together, forming a temporary plug.
  3. Clotting cascade: The platelet plug triggers a series of chemical reactions involving clotting factors (proteins in blood).
  4. Fibrin mesh: Soluble fibrinogen (a plasma protein) is converted to insoluble fibrin threads.
  5. Clot formation: Fibrin threads trap RBCs and form a firm clot that seals the wound.
  6. Clot retraction and dissolution: After healing, the clot is dissolved by plasmin.

Key requirements: Calcium ions, Vitamin K, and all clotting factors must be present. Haemophilia occurs when clotting factor VIII or IX is missing.

4. How can a teacher use the topic of blood groups to teach health awareness in primary school?

Classroom approach:

  • Real-life connection: Ask students if they know their blood group. Explain that knowing it can save lives in emergencies. Encourage students to ask parents.
  • Role play: One group plays "blood bank staff", another plays "patients needing blood". They must match blood groups correctly before "transfusion".
  • Poster activity: Students create an awareness poster about voluntary blood donation with slogans like "Give blood, save a life".
  • Discussion: Why is it important to donate blood? Who can donate? (Any healthy adult, 18–65 years, with adequate haemoglobin levels, every 3 months for men, 4 months for women.)
  • Hygiene link: Discuss why blood should never be shared without proper testing — connection to AIDS and Hepatitis B prevention.

Value: Students learn both science concepts and responsible health citizenship simultaneously.
