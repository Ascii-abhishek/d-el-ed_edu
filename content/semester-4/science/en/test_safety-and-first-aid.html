Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. What is first aid? What are its three main aims?

First aid is the immediate, temporary help given to an injured or sick person before professional medical treatment is available. Its three main aims are: (1) Preserve life — prevent death by maintaining breathing and circulation; (2) Prevent worsening — stop the condition from becoming more serious; (3) Promote recovery — reduce suffering and help the person recover faster.

2. Write the degrees of burns and the first aid for second-degree burns.

Degrees: 1st degree (superficial) — epidermis only, red and painful; 2nd degree (partial thickness) — epidermis and dermis, blisters and very painful; 3rd degree (full thickness) — all skin layers destroyed, no pain.

First aid for 2nd degree burns: (1) Cool immediately with running water for 10–20 minutes. (2) Do NOT burst blisters. (3) Cover with a clean sterile non-stick dressing. (4) Do NOT apply butter, toothpaste, or ice. (5) Take to hospital if the area is large (>5 cm), on face/hands/feet/genitals, or in a child.

3. List five items that should be in a school first aid box and their uses.
  • Sterile gauze pads: Covering and dressing wounds to prevent infection.
  • Triangular bandage: Making slings to support fractured arms.
  • Antiseptic solution (Dettol/iodine): Cleaning wounds to prevent infection.
  • Paracetamol: Relieving pain and reducing fever.
  • Disposable gloves: Protecting the first-aider from contact with blood-borne infections.
4. What is the first aid for a suspected fracture? What must you NOT do?

First aid for fracture: (1) Keep the person still — do not move unless there is immediate danger. (2) Immobilise the fracture using a splint (padded newspaper, wooden stick) or sling. (3) Apply a cold pack/ice wrapped in cloth to reduce swelling. (4) Elevate the injured limb if possible. (5) Get to hospital for X-ray and medical treatment.

Do NOT: Try to straighten or set the bone; move a person with possible neck/spine fracture; remove embedded objects from a wound; delay getting professional help.

5. Write four electrical safety rules for use in the classroom.
  • Never touch electric switches or plugs with wet hands — water conducts electricity.
  • Do not insert objects into electric sockets — risk of electric shock.
  • Switch off and unplug appliances when not in use — prevents overheating and fire risk.
  • Never run electrical cords under carpets or rugs — they can overheat and cause fire.
  • Keep liquids away from electrical equipment — water and electricity are a dangerous combination.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Explain the DRABC protocol in detail with what to do at each step.

DRABC is the primary survey in first aid — a systematic check performed on any injured or unresponsive person:

  • D — Danger: Before approaching, check for any hazards — gas leaks, traffic, fire, live electric wires, unstable structures. Ensure the scene is safe for you and bystanders. Your safety is the first priority.
  • R — Response: Gently shake the person's shoulders and call loudly: "Are you okay? Can you hear me?" A conscious person will respond. If no response → unconscious or unresponsive.
  • A — Airway: Tilt the head back gently (head-tilt chin-lift manoeuvre) to open the airway. Look in the mouth — remove any visible obstruction (food, vomit, loose teeth) with a finger sweep. (Caution: do NOT move head if spinal injury suspected.)
  • B — Breathing: With airway open, look (watch chest rise), listen (ear near mouth), feel (air on cheek) for breathing for up to 10 seconds. If breathing → recovery position. If not breathing → give 2 rescue breaths.
  • C — Circulation: Check carotid pulse (2 fingers on the neck beside the Adam's apple) for up to 10 seconds. If no pulse → start CPR (30 compressions + 2 rescue breaths). Call emergency services.
2. Describe CPR procedure step by step for adults and explain why it is important.

Cardiopulmonary Resuscitation (CPR) is performed when a person has no pulse and/or is not breathing. It artificially circulates blood and delivers oxygen until the heart restarts or medical help arrives.

Procedure (Adult CPR):

  1. Ensure the person is on a firm, flat surface.
  2. Kneel beside the person's chest.
  3. Place the heel of one hand on the centre of the chest (lower half of sternum), other hand on top, fingers interlaced.
  4. Press down at least 5 cm (about 2 inches) at rate of 100–120 compressions per minute. Allow full chest recoil after each compression.
  5. After 30 compressions, give 2 rescue breaths: tilt head back, lift chin, pinch nose, create seal with mouth, blow until chest rises (1 second each breath).
  6. Repeat cycle: 30 compressions + 2 breaths continuously.
  7. Continue until: person regains consciousness and breathes, trained help arrives, or you are exhausted.

Importance: Brain cells begin dying within 4–6 minutes of no blood flow. CPR maintains oxygen delivery and buys critical time for heart to restart or for defibrillation.

3. Design a first aid lesson plan for primary school students (Class 4–5 level).

Topic: Basic First Aid for Minor Injuries

Learning objectives: Students can: (1) identify what first aid is; (2) respond correctly to a minor wound, burn, and nosebleed; (3) know when to call an adult.

Lesson (45 minutes):

Opening (10 min): Ask: "What would you do if your friend fell and cut their hand?" Collect responses. Explain what first aid means in simple language.

Core content (15 min): With a picture chart, show three situations:

  • Small cut: Rinse with clean water → apply pressure with clean cloth → cover with bandage → tell a teacher.
  • Burn: Cool with running water 10 min → do not put butter → cover loosely → tell adult.
  • Nosebleed: Sit up, lean slightly forward (not back) → pinch the soft part of nose for 10 min → breathe through mouth → do not tilt head back.

Practice (15 min): In pairs, students role-play the scenarios using fake materials (cloth as bandage). Teacher observes and corrects.

Closing (5 min): Emphasise: "Always tell an adult — you are helping, not replacing the doctor."

4. What safety precautions should be followed in a school science laboratory?

Personal safety:

  • Always wear safety glasses when conducting experiments with chemicals or heat.
  • Wear closed-toe shoes — protect against chemical spills.
  • Tie back long hair and avoid loose clothing near flames.
  • Never eat or drink in the laboratory.

Chemical safety:

  • Never smell chemicals directly — waft vapour toward nose.
  • Never mix chemicals without teacher instruction.
  • If chemical contacts skin → flush immediately with water for 15 minutes.
  • Label all chemicals clearly; store acids away from bases.

Electrical and fire safety:

  • Keep water away from electrical equipment.
  • Know the location of fire extinguisher, fire blanket, and first aid box.
  • Conduct fire drill at least once a term.
  • Never leave a lit Bunsen burner unattended.

General rules:

  • Walk, do not run, in the laboratory.
  • Report any accident or breakage to the teacher immediately.
  • Clean up spills immediately; dispose of broken glass in a marked container.
