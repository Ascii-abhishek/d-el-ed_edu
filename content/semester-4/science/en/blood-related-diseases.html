Blood-related Diseases

High-scoring chapter in UP D.El.Ed exams 2022–2025. Focus on: anaemia, haemophilia, malaria (vector and transmission), AIDS (what spreads it and what does NOT), Hepatitis B, dengue, and prevention strategies.

Anaemia

  • Definition: Condition in which haemoglobin level or RBC count is below normal.
  • Normal Hb: Men >13 g/dL; Women >12 g/dL; Children >11 g/dL.
  • Types:
    • Iron-deficiency anaemia (most common) — poor diet, worms, heavy bleeding.
    • Vitamin B₁₂/Folic acid deficiency — affects RBC maturation.
    • Haemolytic anaemia — RBCs destroyed faster than produced.
    • Sickle-cell anaemia — genetic; abnormal haemoglobin shape.
  • Symptoms: Weakness, fatigue, pale skin, pale inside eyelids, breathlessness, dizziness, rapid heartbeat.
  • Prevention & treatment: Iron-rich foods (spinach, jaggery, beans, meat, eggs), iron supplements, treating underlying cause.

Haemophilia

  • Definition: Hereditary (genetic) disorder in which blood does not clot properly.
  • Cause: Missing or defective clotting factor — Factor VIII (Haemophilia A) or Factor IX (Haemophilia B).
  • Inheritance: X-linked recessive — mostly affects males; females are usually carriers.
  • Symptoms: Prolonged bleeding from cuts, spontaneous bleeding into joints (haemarthrosis) and muscles, large bruises.
  • Treatment: Infusion of the missing clotting factor; careful lifestyle.
  • Famous example: Queen Victoria of England was a carrier; her descendants across European royalty had haemophilia.

Leukemia (Blood Cancer)

  • Definition: Cancer of blood-forming tissues causing abnormal, uncontrolled production of WBCs.
  • Abnormal WBCs crowd out normal RBCs and platelets.
  • Types: Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL) — most common in children; Chronic Myeloid Leukemia (CML).
  • Symptoms: Anaemia (low RBC), frequent infections (abnormal WBCs don't function), easy bruising (low platelets), bone pain, fatigue, swollen lymph nodes.
  • Treatment: Chemotherapy, radiation, bone marrow transplant.

Malaria

  • Pathogen: Plasmodium — a protozoan parasite. Most dangerous species: P. falciparum.
  • Vector: Female Anopheles mosquito (only female mosquitoes feed on blood).
  • Transmission: Infected female Anopheles bites human → injects Plasmodium sporozoites into blood.
  • Life cycle: Parasite multiplies in liver, then in RBCs → RBCs burst releasing more parasites + toxins → causes fever.
  • Symptoms: Cyclic fever and chills (every 48 hours for P. vivax, every 72 hours for P. malariae), sweating, headache, vomiting, muscle pain.
  • Diagnosis: Blood smear test, Rapid Diagnostic Test (RDT).
  • Treatment: Antimalarial drugs — chloroquine, artemisinin-based combination therapy (ACT).
  • Prevention: Mosquito nets (insecticide-treated), insect repellents, drain stagnant water, spray insecticides, protective clothing at dusk and dawn.

Dengue

  • Pathogen: Dengue virus (4 serotypes: DEN-1, DEN-2, DEN-3, DEN-4) — RNA virus, Flavivirus family.
  • Vector: Aedes aegypti mosquito — bites during daytime (unlike Anopheles which bites at dusk/dawn).
  • Aedes mosquito breeds in clean, stagnant water in containers, tyres, flower pots.
  • Symptoms: Sudden high fever (39–40°C), severe headache and eye pain, joint and muscle pain ("break-bone fever"), rash, platelet count drops sharply.
  • Warning signs (serious dengue): Persistent vomiting, bleeding from gums or nose, blood in vomit/stool, severe abdominal pain.
  • Treatment: No specific antiviral; supportive care — fluids, paracetamol (NOT aspirin or ibuprofen — they can cause bleeding). Platelet transfusion if count very low.
  • Prevention: Remove water-collecting containers weekly, use mosquito nets and repellents, wear full-sleeve clothes.

AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome)

  • Pathogen: Human Immunodeficiency Virus (HIV).
  • Target: CD4+ T-helper lymphocytes — key immune cells. When CD4 count drops below 200/mm³, AIDS is diagnosed.

  • Stages: Acute HIV (flu-like, 2–4 weeks) → Clinical latency (years, may show no symptoms) → AIDS (CD4 < 200, opportunistic infections like TB, pneumonia, fungal infections).
  • Prevention: Safe sex (condoms), test blood before transfusion, sterile needles, prevent mother-to-child (antiretroviral therapy during pregnancy).
  • Treatment: Antiretroviral Therapy (ART) — does not cure HIV but suppresses virus, prevents AIDS stage, allows near-normal life. Must be taken lifelong.

Hepatitis B

  • Pathogen: Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) — infects liver cells.
  • Transmission: Same routes as HIV — blood, sexual contact, mother to newborn. NOT through food/water/casual contact.
  • Incubation period: 45–180 days.
  • Symptoms: Jaundice (yellow skin and eyes), dark urine, pale stools, fatigue, loss of appetite, abdominal pain.
  • Acute vs Chronic: Most adults recover fully (acute); 5–10% become chronic carriers → risk of cirrhosis and liver cancer.
  • Prevention: Vaccination (HBV vaccine) — very effective; 3-dose schedule; given to newborns at birth, 6 weeks, and 6 months. Screen donated blood.
  • Treatment: Antiviral drugs for chronic cases; no cure; liver transplant for severe cirrhosis.

One-minute Revision

  • Anaemia = low Hb; most common cause = iron deficiency.
  • Haemophilia = genetic; X-linked; clotting factor missing; affects mostly males.
  • Malaria = female Anopheles mosquito → Plasmodium → cyclic fever.
  • Dengue = Aedes aegypti (daytime) → platelet drop; NO aspirin.
  • AIDS = HIV → CD4 cells destroyed; transmitted by blood/sex/needles/mother-child; NOT by mosquitoes or casual contact.
  • Hepatitis B = HBV; prevented by vaccine; infects liver; transmitted like HIV.

Common Exam Lines

  • Malaria is caused by Plasmodium, a protozoan, transmitted by the female Anopheles mosquito.
  • HIV is not transmitted by mosquito bites or casual contact such as handshakes or sharing food.
  • Haemophilia is a sex-linked (X-linked) recessive disorder that mostly affects males.
  • Hepatitis B can be prevented by vaccination; Hepatitis A is transmitted by contaminated food and water (unlike HBV).
