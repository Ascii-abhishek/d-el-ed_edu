Evolution: Remember in 7 Points

  • Evolution = gradual change in living organisms over a very long time (thousands to millions of years).
  • It happens through many generations, not within a single organism's life.
  • Individuals with useful traits survive and reproduce more (natural selection).
  • Useful traits are inherited by offspring — unhelpful traits die out.
  • Evolution explains why life is so diverse today.
  • Darwin's Theory (1859): Evolution by natural selection — published in "On the Origin of Species".
  • Evidence: Fossil records, comparative anatomy (homologous organs), embryology, biogeography, molecular biology (DNA similarity).

Darwin's Natural Selection — 4 Key Ideas

  • Variation: Individuals in a population differ slightly from each other.
  • Inheritance: Traits are passed from parent to offspring.
  • Selection: Environment selects individuals with advantageous traits to survive and reproduce more.
  • Time: Over many generations, the selected traits accumulate → new species emerge.

Food Chain

  • Shows who eats whom in a straight line.
  • Energy flows in one direction only: Producer → Consumer.
  • At every step, energy is lost as heat (only about 10% passes to next level — 10% energy rule).
  • Arrow shows direction of energy flow (from eaten to eater).
Sun → Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle

Food Web

  • A food web is a network of many food chains.
  • It is more realistic than a single food chain.
  • One organism may eat many different things and be eaten by many predators.
  • If one organism disappears, the whole web is disturbed (keystone species concept).
     Grass ——→ Grasshopper ——→ Frog ——→ Snake
       |              |              |
       |              ↓              ↓
       ↓          Small bird ——→  Eagle
      Goat

10% Energy Rule

  • Only 10% of energy from one trophic level passes to the next.
  • Rest (90%) is used for respiration, movement, heat.
  • This is why ecosystems can support many more herbivores than carnivores.
  • Example: If grass has 10,000 J → Grasshopper gets 1,000 J → Frog gets 100 J → Snake gets 10 J.

School Garden Ecosystem Example

  • Producers: Grass, neem tree, flowering plants.
  • Primary consumers: Ants, butterflies, caterpillars, goats.
  • Secondary consumers: Lizards, birds eating insects.
  • Decomposers: Fungi and bacteria in soil breaking dead leaves.
  • Abiotic: Sunlight, rainwater, soil, air temperature.
  • Ask students: Draw arrows from food to eater. Where does energy start?

One-minute Revision

  • Evolution = slow change over many generations through natural selection.
  • Adaptation = useful feature for survival in that environment.
  • Ecosystem = biotic (living) + abiotic (non-living) components interacting.
  • Producers make food; consumers eat; decomposers recycle.
  • Food chain is linear; food web is interconnected — more stable.
  • Pyramid of energy is always upright (energy always decreases going up).
  • 10% rule: Only 10% energy passes from one trophic level to the next.

Common Exam Lines

  • An ecosystem is a functional unit of nature where living and non-living components interact with each other.
  • Food chain represents the transfer of food energy from producer to consumers in a linear sequence.
  • Decomposers maintain nutrient cycling in the ecosystem by breaking down dead organic matter.
  • The pyramid of energy is always upright because energy decreases at each successive trophic level.
  • Darwin's theory of natural selection explains how organisms with beneficial variations survive and pass those traits to offspring.
