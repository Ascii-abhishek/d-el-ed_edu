Section 2: Short Answer Questions (5 Questions)

1. State Ohm's law and write its formula.

Ohm's law states that at constant temperature, the electric current through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference (voltage) across it. Formula: V = I × R, where V = voltage (volts), I = current (amperes), R = resistance (ohms).

3. What is the difference between a conductor and an insulator? Give one example of each.

A conductor allows electric current to flow through it easily because it has free electrons. Example: copper wire. An insulator does not allow current to flow because its electrons are tightly bound. Example: rubber coating on wires.

4. What is electric power? Write its formula and unit.

Electric power is the rate at which electrical energy is consumed or converted. Formula: P = V × I (also P = I²R = V²/R). Unit: Watt (W). 1000 W = 1 kilowatt (kW).

5. Why should you never touch electric switches with wet hands?

Water is a conductor of electricity (because it contains dissolved salts and minerals). Touching a switch with wet hands creates a conducting path between the live electrical parts and the human body, which can cause electric shock or even death.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Explain the heating effect of electric current with examples and its applications.

Heating effect: When electric current flows through a resistance wire, the electrical energy is converted into heat energy. This is called the Joule heating effect.

Joule's law: Heat produced H = I²Rt (I = current, R = resistance, t = time).

Factors: More heat produced when resistance is higher, current is larger, or time is longer.

Applications:

  • Electric heater — nichrome wire has high resistance → produces lots of heat.
  • Electric iron — heating element converts electricity to heat for pressing clothes.
  • Toaster — bread is toasted by radiation from hot nichrome coils.
  • Incandescent bulb — tungsten filament becomes white-hot and emits light.
  • Fuse wire — uses this principle for safety: excess current melts it.
2. Compare series and parallel circuits with diagrams and explain why homes use parallel wiring.

Series Circuit:

Battery — R1 — R2 — R3 — (back to battery)
  • Same current flows through all components.
  • Voltage divides: V = V₁ + V₂ + V₃.
  • Total resistance: R = R₁ + R₂ + R₃ (increases).
  • If one component fails, all fail.

Parallel Circuit:

Battery — R1 |
              R2 | — (back to battery)
              R3 |
  • Same voltage across all branches.
  • Current divides: I = I₁ + I₂ + I₃.
  • Total resistance decreases (1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃).
  • Each branch works independently.

Why homes use parallel: Each appliance gets full voltage (220V), they work independently, and one failure doesn't affect others. Also, individual switches can control individual appliances.

3. Describe the three wires in domestic electrical wiring and explain the importance of earthing.

Three wires in domestic wiring:

WireColourFunction
Live (Phase)Red or BrownCarries current from supply to appliance
NeutralBlack or BlueReturns current from appliance back to supply
EarthGreen or Yellow-GreenSafety wire connected to earth/ground

Importance of earthing: If a fault develops and the metal body of an appliance becomes live, without earthing the user would get a shock on touching it. With earthing, the excess current flows through the earth wire (low resistance path) to the ground, tripping the fuse or MCB. This prevents electric shock.

4. Design a classroom activity to demonstrate the difference between conductors and insulators using simple materials.

Activity: Testing Electrical Conductors and Insulators

Materials needed: One 9V battery, one small LED or buzzer, two wires with crocodile clips, various test objects (copper wire, iron nail, aluminium foil, pencil graphite, rubber eraser, plastic ruler, piece of wood, glass marble, water in a cup).

Circuit setup:

Battery (+) → wire → [test object] → wire → LED → Battery (−)

Procedure: Touch crocodile clips to each test object one at a time. If LED glows → conductor. If LED stays dark → insulator.

Recording table:

ObjectLED glows?Conductor/Insulator
Copper wireYesConductor
Rubber eraserNoInsulator
Pencil graphiteDimly yesConductor (non-metal exception!)

Learning: Students discover graphite (non-metal) conducts electricity — an important exception. They connect learning to daily objects instead of abstract memorisation.
