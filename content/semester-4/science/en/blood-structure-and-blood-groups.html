Blood Structure and Blood Groups

Scoring chapter — exams regularly ask about blood components, ABO system, universal donor/recipient, and Rh factor. Tables and comparisons work best here.

Blood Plasma

  • Makes up about 55% of total blood volume.
  • Contains: water (90%), proteins (albumin, globulin, fibrinogen), nutrients, hormones, gases, waste products.
  • Serum = plasma without clotting proteins (fibrinogen removed after clotting).
  • Functions: transport, maintaining pH, regulating body temperature, immune defence (antibodies).

Red Blood Cells (RBCs / Erythrocytes)

  • Shape: Biconcave disc — this increases surface area for oxygen exchange.
  • No nucleus in mature human RBCs — provides more space for haemoglobin.
  • Contain haemoglobin (iron-containing protein) that binds oxygen and gives blood its red colour.
  • Lifespan: 120 days — old RBCs destroyed in spleen and liver.
  • New RBCs made in red bone marrow.
  • Normal count: 4.5–5.5 million per mm³ (men); 4.0–5.0 million per mm³ (women).
  • Low RBC/haemoglobin → Anaemia.

  • High WBC = infection or leukaemia; Low WBC = immune deficiency.

Platelets (Thrombocytes)

  • Tiny fragments of large cells called megakaryocytes.
  • No nucleus.
  • Lifespan: 7–10 days.
  • Normal count: 1,50,000–4,00,000 per mm³.
  • Role: Blood clotting (haemostasis) — platelets gather at wound and form a plug, then trigger clotting cascade to form fibrin mesh.
  • Low platelets (thrombocytopenia) → bleeding risk; high in dengue.

  • Universal Donor: O group — no antigens on RBCs, so no rejection.
  • Universal Recipient: AB group — no antibodies in plasma, so no reaction.
  • Mismatched transfusion → agglutination (clumping) → life-threatening reaction.

Rh Factor

  • Discovered by Landsteiner and Wiener in Rhesus monkeys.
  • Rh positive (Rh+): D antigen present on RBC surface (~85% of population).
  • Rh negative (Rh−): D antigen absent (~15% of population).
  • If Rh− blood given to Rh+ person → usually no immediate problem.
  • If Rh+ blood given to Rh− person → anti-Rh antibodies form; second exposure causes transfusion reaction.
  • Erythroblastosis fetalis: Rh− mother + Rh+ father → Rh+ baby. During delivery, baby's blood enters mother. Mother forms anti-Rh antibodies. In next Rh+ pregnancy, mother's antibodies attack fetal RBCs. Prevented by Anti-D injection (RhoGAM).

Blood Bank

  • Facility that collects, tests, stores, and supplies blood and blood products.
  • Donated blood is tested for HIV, Hepatitis B, Hepatitis C, Syphilis, Malaria before storage.
  • Whole blood stored at 2–6°C for up to 35–42 days.
  • Plasma frozen separately and stored longer.
  • Safe voluntary blood donation: every 3 months for men, 4 months for women (minimum 450 ml per donation).

  • Vitamin K is essential for clotting factor production.
  • Calcium ions needed for clotting.
  • Haemophilia — clotting factor VIII or IX missing → uncontrolled bleeding.

One-minute Revision

  • Blood = Plasma (55%) + RBCs + WBCs + Platelets.
  • RBC: biconcave, no nucleus, carries O₂, lives 120 days.
  • WBC: nucleus, irregular, fights infection.
  • Platelets: tiny fragments, helps clotting.
  • Universal Donor: O group; Universal Recipient: AB group.
  • Rh− mother + Rh+ baby → risk of erythroblastosis fetalis; prevented by Anti-D injection.

Common Exam Lines

  • Haemoglobin is the iron-containing protein in RBCs that transports oxygen.
  • Blood group O is called universal donor because its RBCs have no A or B antigens.
  • Platelets help in blood clotting by forming a plug at the site of injury.
  • Rh factor must be matched especially for repeated transfusions and during pregnancy.
