2. Write four routes through which HIV is NOT transmitted.

HIV is NOT transmitted through: (1) mosquito or insect bites; (2) casual physical contact such as handshake, hug, or sitting next to an infected person; (3) sharing food, water, or utensils; (4) coughing, sneezing, or breathing the same air; (5) saliva, sweat, or tears.

3. What is anaemia? Write its symptoms and two ways to prevent it.

Anaemia: A condition where haemoglobin or RBC count falls below the normal level, reducing oxygen-carrying capacity of blood.

Symptoms: Weakness, fatigue, pale skin, pale inner eyelids, breathlessness on exertion, dizziness, rapid heartbeat.

Prevention: (1) Eat iron-rich foods — green leafy vegetables, jaggery, beans, eggs, meat. (2) Take iron and folic acid supplements, especially during pregnancy. (3) Treat intestinal worm infestations that cause blood loss.

4. What is leukaemia? How does it differ from normal increase in WBC count?

Leukaemia is a cancer of blood-forming tissues (bone marrow) in which abnormal, non-functional WBCs are produced in uncontrolled numbers. These cells cannot fight infections effectively and crowd out normal RBCs and platelets. A normal increase in WBC count (leukocytosis) during an infection is a healthy immune response — the cells are functional and numbers return to normal after recovery. In leukaemia, the increase is permanent, uncontrolled, and the cells are malformed.

5. What is ART (Antiretroviral Therapy)? Why is it not a cure for AIDS?

ART (Antiretroviral Therapy) is a combination of drugs that suppresses HIV replication in the body. It reduces the viral load (number of HIV copies) to undetectable levels and allows the CD4 count to recover, preventing the progression to AIDS. ART is not a cure because it does not eliminate HIV from the body — it only controls it. The virus hides in "reservoirs" (latently infected cells) and cannot be completely eradicated with current medicines. ART must be taken lifelong without stopping.

Section 3: Long Answer Questions (4 Questions)

1. Explain HIV/AIDS: transmission, stages, prevention, and the importance of reducing stigma.

HIV (Human Immunodeficiency Virus) causes AIDS (Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome).

Transmission routes:

  • Unprotected sexual contact with infected person.
  • Transfusion of infected blood or blood products.
  • Sharing needles or syringes with an infected person.
  • From HIV+ mother to child during pregnancy, delivery, or breastfeeding.

Stages:

  1. Acute HIV: 2–4 weeks after infection; flu-like symptoms; high viral load.
  2. Clinical latency: Virus active but slow; may last years; mild or no symptoms.
  3. AIDS: CD4 count below 200/mm³; severe immune deficiency; opportunistic infections (TB, pneumonia, fungal).

Prevention: Safe sex (condoms), tested blood, sterile needles, ART during pregnancy to prevent mother-child transmission.

Reducing stigma: HIV is not transmitted by casual contact. People with HIV can live and work normally. Stigma prevents people from getting tested and treated — teachers must correct myths ("don't share food with HIV+ people") to encourage testing and care.

2. Describe the life cycle of the malaria parasite in the human body and how it causes symptoms.

Life cycle of Plasmodium in humans:

  1. Infected female Anopheles mosquito bites human → injects sporozoites (malaria parasites) into blood.
  2. Sporozoites travel to liver → multiply inside liver cells → form merozoites (takes 5–16 days: incubation period).
  3. Merozoites released into bloodstream → invade Red Blood Cells (RBCs).
  4. Inside RBCs, parasites multiply → RBCs burst → releasing more merozoites + toxins into blood.
  5. Bursting of RBCs causes cyclic fever and chills (every 48 hrs for P. vivax; every 72 hrs for P. malariae).
  6. Some merozoites develop into sexual forms (gametocytes) → if another mosquito bites → parasite completes sexual cycle in mosquito → new sporozoites form → cycle repeats.

Symptoms: Cyclic fever corresponding to RBC bursting cycles; chills, sweating, headache, muscle pain, nausea, anaemia (RBCs destroyed), and spleen enlargement.

3. How would you teach primary school students about prevention of mosquito-borne diseases?

Teaching strategy for primary school:

Concept introduction (simple language): "Some mosquitoes carry tiny germs. When they bite us, these germs enter our blood and make us very sick. We can stop this by not letting mosquitoes breed near our home."

Key prevention messages:

  • Do not let water collect in tyres, pots, or coolers — change water every 3 days or cover containers.
  • Sleep under a mosquito net at night (important for malaria).
  • Wear full-sleeve clothes during evening.
  • Use mosquito repellent (age-appropriate).
  • Keep surroundings clean and drains clear.

Activity: "Mosquito patrol" — students walk around the school and identify any containers with stagnant water. Report and arrange to drain them. This builds community responsibility.

Song/slogan: Create a simple rhyme about draining water weekly — easier for young children to remember.

4. What is Hepatitis B? Compare its transmission with Hepatitis A. Explain how it is prevented.

Hepatitis B: A liver infection caused by the Hepatitis B Virus (HBV). It infects liver cells, causing inflammation. Chronic infection can lead to cirrhosis (liver scarring) and liver cancer.

FeatureHepatitis AHepatitis B
PathogenHepatitis A Virus (HAV)Hepatitis B Virus (HBV)
TransmissionContaminated food and water (fecal-oral route)Blood, sexual contact, mother to child
PreventionSafe water, sanitation, HAV vaccineHBV vaccine, blood screening, sterile equipment
OutcomeAlmost always acute; self-limiting; full recoveryCan become chronic; risk of cirrhosis and cancer

Prevention of Hepatitis B:

  • Vaccination: 3-dose HBV vaccine — most effective prevention. Given to all newborns in India.
  • Screen donated blood for HBV before transfusion.
  • Use sterile needles for injections; never share.
  • Safe sex practices.
  • Pregnant women with HBV should receive antivirals to prevent transmission to newborn.
