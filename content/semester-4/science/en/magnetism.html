Properties of Magnets

  • A freely suspended magnet always aligns in the North-South direction (used in compass).
  • Like poles (N-N or S-S) repel each other.
  • Unlike poles (N-S) attract each other.
  • Magnetic force acts at a distance — no direct contact needed.
  • When a magnet is cut into pieces, each piece becomes a new complete magnet with its own N and S pole.
  • Magnetic materials: iron, nickel, cobalt (ferromagnetic).
  • Non-magnetic materials: wood, plastic, glass, copper, aluminium.

Magnetic Field Lines

  • Invisible lines showing direction and strength of magnetic field.
  • Lines travel from N to S outside the magnet, and S to N inside.
  • Closer field lines = stronger field.
  • Lines never cross each other.
  • Lines are most concentrated near the poles.

Earth's Magnetism

  • Earth behaves like a giant bar magnet — its magnetic field is caused by convection currents in molten iron in the outer core.
  • Geographic north pole ≠ Magnetic north pole — they are about 11° apart.
  • Earth's magnetic south pole is near geographic north pole (that is why the N-pole of a compass points toward geographic north).

Electromagnets

  • Made by winding many turns of insulated copper wire around a soft iron core and passing electric current.
  • The magnetism exists only when current flows and stops when current is switched off.
  • Strength can be controlled by: (1) increasing current; (2) increasing number of turns; (3) using a better core material.

Classroom Activity

  • Give students a bar magnet and a sheet of paper with iron filings. Let them observe field line patterns.
  • Make a simple compass using a needle, magnet (stroke 50 times), and water with a leaf float.
  • Build a simple electromagnet using a 9V battery, copper wire, and iron nail — test how many paper clips it can lift vs. number of wire turns.

One-minute Revision

  • Like poles repel; unlike poles attract.
  • Freely suspended magnet → aligns N-S direction.
  • Earth's magnetic south pole is near geographic north (that's why compass N-pole points north).
  • Declination = geographic N vs magnetic N; Dip = 0° at equator, 90° at poles.
  • Electromagnet: ON when current flows, OFF when not. Strength adjustable.

Common Exam Lines

  • A magnet always has two poles; it is impossible to have a monopole.
  • Soft iron is used as the core of an electromagnet because it gains and loses magnetism quickly.
  • The angle of dip is 90° at the magnetic poles and 0° at the magnetic equator.
  • Magnetic declination is the angle between true geographic north and magnetic north at any given place.
